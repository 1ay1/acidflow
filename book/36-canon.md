# 36 · Listening studies: the canon annotated

The best way to learn acid is to listen to it — critically, repeatedly, and with attention to what each element is doing. This chapter annotates ten canonical acid records, explaining what the producers did and how to hear it.

Each record is examined with these lenses:

- **What's happening in the 303**: pattern, accents, slides, filter motion.
- **What's happening in the drums**: kick, snare, hats, clap — which are present, which aren't.
- **What's happening in the arrangement**: intro, builds, drops, breakdowns.
- **What to listen for**: specific moments that teach something.

You should listen to each record (they're all streaming/YouTube easily accessible) while reading. This chapter is hollow without the listening.

## 1. Phuture — Acid Tracks (1987)

**Length**: 11:50. **BPM**: ~122.

This is the record that invented acid. DJ Pierre, Spanky, and Herb J had a TB-303 they didn't know how to use properly. One night, fiddling with it, they hit on the filter-sweep pattern. Marshall Jefferson got a copy, put it on his demo tape, people lost their minds.

**303**: A pedal pattern on C. Mostly C's with occasional C one octave up. Slides appear on the octave-up notes. Accents are sparse. The pattern itself is extraordinarily simple — you can program it in under a minute.

**Drums**: Kick on every beat. Clap on 2 and 4. That's it. No hats (mostly), no snare.

**Arrangement**: 12 minutes of the same pattern. What changes is the filter. For the first 3 minutes, CUTOFF is low — the pattern is muffled. Slowly it opens. By minute 6, the filter is full open and the squelch is everywhere. Then slowly closing again.

**What to listen for**:
- The filter sweep is slow — minutes, not bars. You have to listen at minute-scale to hear it move.
- Each accent punctuates. Without the accents, the sweep would be monotone. With them, your ear tracks the emphasis.
- There's no breakdown, no drop. The arrangement is one long continuous motion.

**acidflow to replicate**: Load preset 1 (Acid Tracks). Let it play. Slowly rotate CUTOFF over 10 minutes, from ~20% up to ~80% and back. That's the track.

## 2. A Guy Called Gerald — Voodoo Ray (1988)

**Length**: 4:13. **BPM**: ~120.

UK acid at its peak. Gerald used an MC-202 (not a 303), but the filter character is close enough that the same techniques apply.

**303** (-ish): A minor pentatonic riff. A-C-D-E-A pattern, with some chromatic bits. Slides between notes. "Sung" character — RES is below 50%, so it doesn't scream; it *sings*.

**Drums**: Kick, clap on backbeat, hats on offbeats. Same as acid house. But with layered sounds — a second kick drum layered on top, an extra clap snap.

**Arrangement**: Intro with vocals. Main section with the 303 line. Breakdown. Return. Outro.

**What to listen for**:
- The vocals are part of the composition. Acid-vocal as a subgenre exists because of this record.
- The 303 melody is *memorable*. It's a hook, not a texture. That's UK acid's contribution — making the 303 line a melody, not just a filter toy.
- The drums are warmer than Chicago's — more midrange, less clinical.

**acidflow to replicate**: Load preset 5 (Voodoo Ray). RES at 40%. CUTOFF at 55%. Drums: clap on 5, 13, kick on every beat.

## 3. Hardfloor — Acperience 1 (1992)

**Length**: 7:40. **BPM**: ~135.

Hardfloor's signature. Ramon Zenker and Oliver Bondzio used three 303 clones (x0xb0x era) layered. This is where acid gets AGGRESSIVE.

**303**: A minor pedal with occasional octave-up slides. Heavy accents on beats 1 and 5. RES is maxed — the filter is self-oscillating on accented notes. The "scream" is famous.

**Drums**: Pounding kick on every beat. Snare on backbeat. 16th-note hats. Open hat fills. It's busy.

**Arrangement**: Long intro with the 303 muted. 303 comes in at 1:30. Builds through 2:30. Drop at 3:00. Main section until 5:30. Breakdown, drop 2, outro.

**What to listen for**:
- The screaming accents. On each accented note, the filter self-oscillates briefly, producing a distinctive "scream."
- The layered 303s. You can sometimes hear two different 303 lines at the same time.
- The build is LONG. Dancers have to wait 3 minutes for the drop. Tension.

**acidflow to replicate**: Load preset 3 (Acperience). Knob: Squelch. RES at 90%. CUTOFF low but rising. Drive at 50%.

## 4. Joey Beltram — Energy Flash (1990)

**Length**: 7:30. **BPM**: ~135.

The rave engine. Beltram used the 303 as a pulsing, relentless engine rather than a melodic line. The 303 here plays the same note every step — it's a drone with filter motion.

**303**: Constant E1 on every 16th step. All 16 steps hit. Accents on the downbeats (1, 5, 9, 13). No slides. The "melody" is just the filter opening and closing.

**Drums**: Kick every beat. Clap on backbeat. Hats sparse. The drums are less the point; the 303 drone IS the track.

**Arrangement**: The 303 comes in immediately. It's continuous for 7 minutes. The arrangement is 100% filter motion — slowly opening and closing CUTOFF over the track.

**What to listen for**:
- The pulse. Every 16th is the same note. It's hypnotic.
- The filter opens slowly. At minute 3 it's fully open. This is where Beltram gets the maximum squelch.
- The accent pattern gives rhythmic structure. Without accents, the drone would be boring. With them, you have "beats."

**acidflow to replicate**: Preset 6 (Energy Flash). Pattern: all 16 steps with same pitch, accents on 1/5/9/13. CUTOFF starts at 15%, opens to 90% over the track.

## 5. Josh Wink — Higher State of Consciousness (1995)

**Length**: 9:58. **BPM**: ~135.

The dance-floor version of Acid Tracks. Wink's innovation: the pattern is deliberately simple, but the arrangement is complex. Filter motion is the ENTIRE song.

**303**: A pedal-like pattern on a root (A or thereabouts). Modest accents. Very few slides. Pattern doesn't change for the whole 10 minutes.

**Drums**: Kick, clap, hats. Standard.

**Arrangement**: Long. Intro, first build, first drop, main, breakdown (the famous "acid test" breakdown where the vocal kicks in), second drop, outro. The breakdown is the track's iconic moment.

**What to listen for**:
- The breakdown. Drums disappear, the 303 keeps going with heavy reverb. Vocal sample ("we can only hope you've reached a higher state of consciousness"). Then the drop.
- The filter motion. Even without the breakdown, the filter is sweeping continuously.
- The cleanness. Wink's production is clean — every element distinct, no mud.

**acidflow to replicate**: Preset 2 (Higher State). DECAY long. Slow filter sweeps. Make a dramatic breakdown (mute drums, reverb up).

## 6. Plastikman (Richie Hawtin) — Spastik (1993)

**Length**: 6:29. **BPM**: 138.

Minimal acid. Hawtin's statement: the 303 doesn't need drums. This track is 90% 303 with a minimal kick.

**303**: Sparse, syncopated pattern. Maybe 6-8 hits per bar. Long decays so each note rings. Self-oscillating resonance at times.

**Drums**: Kick on some beats. Mostly no drums at all.

**Arrangement**: Slowly evolving. New drum hits appear, then disappear. 303 pattern morphs gradually. No sudden drops, no clear sections. It just flows.

**What to listen for**:
- The space. Long silences between notes. The listener's ear fills the space.
- The drum entries. When a new drum sound appears, it's an event because there's so little else.
- The filter motion over bars. Very slow.

**acidflow to replicate**: Preset 7 (Plastikman). Sparse pattern. Knob: Dubby. DECAY at max. RES high. Drums almost absent.

## 7. Phuture — We Are Phuture (1988)

**Length**: 7:15. **BPM**: ~120.

The "other" Phuture acid. More minimal than Acid Tracks. Basically just 303 and kick.

**303**: Deep pedal pattern. Low CUTOFF (dark). Gradual filter motion.

**Drums**: Kick and sparse hats. No clap, no snare. This is house music at its leanest.

**Arrangement**: 7 minutes of slow evolution.

**What to listen for**:
- How little is happening. It's almost nothing. And yet it's compelling.
- The kick's presence. With so few other elements, the kick IS the track's rhythmic support.

**acidflow to replicate**: Similar to Acid Tracks but lower CUTOFF and even sparser drums.

## 8. Humanoid — Stakker Humanoid (1988)

**Length**: 3:45. **BPM**: ~125.

FSOL-affiliated, weirder, more playful. Stakker was a TV-era electronic music group.

**303**: Octave-jumping pattern. Notes bounce up and down an octave on every step. Busy and melodic.

**Drums**: Standard acid kit.

**Arrangement**: Fast paced, many sections, vocal samples layered.

**What to listen for**:
- The octave hops. Step 1 = root, step 2 = root+12, step 3 = root, step 4 = root+12, etc. Creates a "zipper" feel.
- The density. Busy compared to Chicago-era.

**acidflow to replicate**: Preset 13 (Humanoid). Pattern with alternating octaves. Moderate filter.

## 9. Adonis — No Way Back (1986)

**Length**: 6:20. **BPM**: ~120.

Pre-Acid Tracks acid. Adonis was programming with a 303 in 1986, before "acid" was a genre label.

**303**: A call-and-response pattern. Step 1-4 = C root. Step 5 = Eb jump. Step 9 = F jump. The line has a riff shape rather than a pure pedal.

**Drums**: Kick, clap, hats. Clean.

**Arrangement**: Medium length, standard verse-chorus.

**What to listen for**:
- The riff. This is the first 303 line with a memorable shape beyond pedal point. Proto-melodic acid.

**acidflow to replicate**: Preset 4 (No Way Back). Call-and-response pattern.

## 10. Frankie Bones — Looney Tunes Vol. 1 (1990)

**Length**: 5:30. **BPM**: ~130.

New York acid-meets-breakbeat. Bones mixed acid with breakbeats (sampled drum loops) — an innovation of the era.

**303**: Dense pattern, heavy accents, some slides. Drives the track.

**Drums**: Classic rave breakbeats (Amen, Funky Drummer samples — not 4-on-floor). acidflow can't sample, so replicate with driving hats and syncopated kicks.

**Arrangement**: Rave structure — long intro, builds, drops, piano-free rave chaos.

**What to listen for**:
- The breakbeats + 303 combination. This template became everything from jungle to breakcore.

**acidflow to replicate**: Heavy 303 pattern. Syncopated drums. Drive heavy.

## How to do a listening study

Pick any record. Listen 5-10 times. Each pass, focus on one element:

1. **Pass 1**: Just listen. Let it wash over you.
2. **Pass 2**: Listen for the 303. Ignore everything else. What's the pattern? How many hits per bar? Accents where?
3. **Pass 3**: Listen for the filter. When does it move? Slow or fast? How much range?
4. **Pass 4**: Listen for drums. Kick pattern? Snare? Hats? Claps?
5. **Pass 5**: Listen for FX. Reverb? Delay? How wet?
6. **Pass 6**: Listen for arrangement. Intro → main → breakdown → outro? How long is each section?

By pass 6, you can accurately describe the record technically. You've internalized the techniques.

Now try to replicate it in acidflow. You won't match perfectly. But you'll learn more from attempting than from listening alone.

## Compiling your own canon

Beyond these 10, every producer has favorites. Build your own list of 20-30 acid records you love. Do listening studies on each.

Over time, you'll develop a vocabulary — specific moves from specific records that you incorporate into your own tracks. This is how producers develop style: not from abstract theory but from internalized examples.

## Don't copy, assimilate

The goal isn't to make tracks that sound exactly like *Acperience 1*. The goal is to understand what Hardfloor was doing well enough that their techniques become part of your toolkit. Then you apply those techniques to your own ideas.

Every acid producer is a student of earlier acid producers. Nobody invented it from scratch. The tradition is built by re-applying, re-interpreting, and occasionally breaking the conventions the canon established.

## Try this

1. Pick ONE record from the list.
2. Listen 5 times with different focuses per pass.
3. In acidflow, try to program a similar pattern.
4. A/B your version with the original. Where do you fall short? Where do you differ intentionally?
5. Repeat with a different record.

After 5 records, you'll have noticeably improved as an acid producer.
