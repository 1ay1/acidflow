# 2 · A short history of the TB-303

You can make acid without knowing any of this. People have been making acid for forty years without knowing any of this. But the history explains why the instrument has the controls it has, why those controls are called what they're called, and why "acid" ended up meaning what it means. Skip if you're impatient.

## 1981: Roland tries to replace a bass player

The early 1980s were a boom time for electronic "companion" instruments — drum machines and bass machines marketed at musicians who wanted to rehearse alone. Roland had already shipped the TR-606 Drumatix, a compact drum machine, in 1981. The **TB-303 Bass Line** was its bass-player counterpart. The TR and TB were sold together: plug one into the other with a DIN sync cable and you had a rhythm section in a briefcase.

The engineering brief was "make a box that sounds like a bass guitar." That brief failed, because a bass guitar has complex harmonics, a human player's touch, string resonance, amplifier interaction, and unpredictable microvariations. A single oscillator through a lowpass filter, controlled by a simple envelope, does not. But the brief didn't *really* fail, because Roland simultaneously overshot: the filter they chose — a 4-pole transistor ladder, essentially a Moog clone with modifications — is capable of self-oscillation, and the envelope circuit on the 303 has a quirk called the *accent capacitor* that stacks across notes in ways the designers did not fully document even internally.

The result was a machine that sounded like a bass guitar only in the loosest sense — a buzzy, resonant, almost vocal thing with a distinct *squelch* quality when the filter was open, and a boring, muted, *dub*-ish thing when it was closed. Neither mode was especially convincing as a guitar bass. Reviewers were polite. Sales were terrible. Roland discontinued the 303 after about eighteen months.

## 1985–1987: The 303 becomes landfill

Because it flopped, the 303 showed up on the secondhand market almost immediately. Musicians dumped them. Music shops stocked them cheap. In Chicago, a city with a large and serious dance-music scene growing out of the post-disco culture Frankie Knuckles and Ron Hardy had built at the Warehouse and the Music Box, dance producers were buying everything they could get their hands on for cheap — Korg MS-10s, Roland JX-3Ps, TR-606s, anything.

At some point — DJ Pierre says "1985 or '86" in most interviews — three teenagers (Nathaniel Pierre Jones, later DJ Pierre; Earl Smith Jr., later Spanky; and Herb Jackson, later Herb J) got hold of a used 303 in a shop in Chicago and took it home. They couldn't figure out the programming interface, which is famously opaque. In the process of trying to program *any* pattern, they left notes in, twisted the cutoff, turned the resonance up, and recorded what came out.

## 1987: *Acid Tracks*

The recording ran for about twelve minutes in the original demo. Ron Hardy, the DJ at the Music Box, played it four times in one night. The crowd reaction was so specific and so ecstatic that bootleg cassette copies started circulating with "Ron Hardy's Acid Trax" written on them in marker — because the sound, people said, was like being on acid. (The drug had nothing to do with the track's production or the producers' intent; it was purely a crowd reaction.)

The record was released on Trax Records in 1987 under the name **Phuture**, and it is — with no real competition — the founding document of acid house. Twelve minutes. One 303 pattern, twisted. A TR-707 underneath. That's it.

The word *acid* stuck. By 1988 it was the name of a genre.

## 1988–1989: London, the Second Summer of Love, and the moral panic

Acid house reached the UK almost immediately. A cluster of producers in London — Mark Moore (S'Express), A Guy Called Gerald (*Voodoo Ray*), Baby Ford, 808 State — built on the Chicago template. Because the 303 was cheap, ubiquitous, and sounded deranged, it became the de facto lead voice of the UK acid scene.

Culturally, the moment was enormous. The Second Summer of Love (1988–89) was the UK equivalent of the Chicago Warehouse scene multiplied by a population and a media machine. The Sun and the Mirror ran front-page stories about "evil acid cult" parties. Parliament passed the Criminal Justice Act. DJs went to jail. 1989 was also the year *Energy Flash* came out (Beltram), which dragged acid into the faster, heavier direction that would become acid techno.

What mattered musically: the UK scene introduced longer filter arcs (the Chicago tracks had tended to be more abrupt), heavier use of the sequencer for single-bar riffs rather than twelve-minute jams, and the integration of the 303 with piano stabs, rave stabs, and breakbeats. *Voodoo Ray* in particular (1988) is one of the cleanest examples of how a 303 riff with restraint — short pattern, clear melody, enormous space around it — can be more compositionally durable than the original Chicago approach.

## 1990–1995: Detroit, Rotterdam, acid techno

The Detroit techno scene, which had always been parallel to Chicago house rather than derived from it, absorbed the 303 into its own logic — more minimal, more machine-facing, more European in affect. Jeff Mills, Robert Hood, and Richie Hawtin (then recording as F.U.S.E. and Plastikman) used the 303 for the resonant *texture* more than the melodic *riff*. Hawtin's *Plus 8* label and his Plastikman albums (*Sheet One*, 1993; *Musik*, 1994) are the clearest examples of what acid sounds like when the melodic content is stripped down to two or three notes and the attention moves entirely to filter and resonance behavior.

In parallel, the Rotterdam scene — fast, industrial, unrepentant — produced what's now called *acid techno*: BPMs of 135+, relentless sequencing, heavy drive, very little restraint. The Stay Up Forever label (London, starting 1994 with Chris Liberator's crew) codified this into a recognizable subgenre. Hardfloor in Germany was doing something related but with more musical ambition — *Acperience 1* (1992) layered three 303s at once, making explicit what earlier acid records had done by accident.

By the mid-90s, the 303 was already out of production, the used-price spiral had started, and the first hardware and software clones were appearing.

## 1995–2005: The clone era

The spike in 303 prices from ~£100 in 1990 to £1000+ by 2000 created the market that software emulation and hardware clones still live in. Propellerheads' ReBirth (1997) was the breakthrough product — a software 303 (and two TR-808 / 909 drum machines) that made acid-style production possible on a laptop for the first time. Hardware clones followed: the Doepfer MS-404, the Syntecno TeeBee, the Future Retro 777, the x0xb0x (an open-source clone; still one of the closest hardware recreations).

None of these fully captured the 303, for reasons we'll dig into in Chapter 6. But they were close enough to keep the genre alive, and the existence of cheap emulation meant acid stopped being a genre defined by hardware scarcity. By the 2000s it was a technique — available to anyone who could run a plugin — and its use scattered across IDM, minimal techno, electroclash, and assorted revival scenes.

## 2005–present: Reissue, pastiche, and renewal

Roland reissued the 303 as the **TB-03** in 2016 (part of the Boutique line) and again as the **TB-303 AIRA Compact** in 2023. Behringer shipped the TD-3 in 2020, a direct analog clone at a low price. Software plugins multiplied — Audiorealism's ABL, D16's Phoscyon, AIR Music's series, and open-source projects. acidflow is a contemporary entry in this tradition.

The sound is, at this point, fully naturalized into pop music. You can hear 303-style acid lines in records by The Chemical Brothers, Daft Punk, Justice, Fred Again, and on essentially any mainstream dance release that wants to signal "retro underground." There is also a living acid techno underground — labels like Stay Up Forever, Planet Rhythm, 99Chants, Nice7 — still pressing vinyl and playing parties.

## Why the TB-303 ended up defining a genre

A few structural reasons worth noting, because they inform every design choice in this book:

1. **It was monophonic and sequence-only.** You couldn't play it like a piano. You *had* to program patterns, and the programming interface was so annoying that most patterns ended up short, aggressive, and full of accidents. Short, aggressive, accidental patterns turn out to be great for dance music.

2. **The filter was unusually resonant.** 303 resonance pushes into self-oscillation more readily than most 4-pole ladders. That extreme behavior — the squeal — is what gives acid its signature tone. A more polite filter would have produced a different genre, or none.

3. **The envelope modulation was exponential.** The 303's cutoff is modulated in V/octave space, which means a decaying envelope produces a characteristic *curve* — fast drop, long tail — rather than the linear ramp other synths of the era produced. This is the "ptoonnnng" shape of every 303 note.

4. **It had an accent.** Not a velocity parameter — a *global accent* that stacked a capacitor across successive accented notes. The behavior is weirdly musical and impossible to emulate with simple velocity tables. We'll spend a whole chapter on it (Chapter 12).

5. **It had slides.** The 303's portamento between slid notes is an exponential RC curve — slower than pitch-bend, faster than chromatic interpolation — and it happens to match the glide speed of a walking bass line in a blues band. This is not coincidence; this is the original design intent ("simulate a bass player") leaking through into the acid era.

Acid was latent in the design. It just needed someone to fail to program it properly for the latency to show up.

## What this means for you

Two things.

One: **the constraints are the point**. The 303 does only a few things. The entire genre is built out of the small number of gestures those few things permit. When you're writing an acid pattern and you feel starved of options, you are in the place where the music lives. Resist the temptation to add more voices, more automation, more layers. The records that have lasted are the ones that stayed inside the 303's constraints and pushed on them, not the ones that escaped.

Two: **acid is a specific tradition, not a vibe**. You can make vague dance music with filtered leads that has nothing to do with acid. To make *acid*, you have to inherit something: the short-loop aesthetic, the resonance-as-melody approach, the accent-as-rhythm, the slide-as-glue, the filter-as-evolution. Every one of those is a chapter in this book. If you absorb them, your tracks will belong to the tradition whether or not they sound exactly like 1987.

That's the history. Onward to theory.
