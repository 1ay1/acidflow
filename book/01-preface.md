# 1 · Preface: Why acid matters

In 1981, a small engineering team at the Roland Corporation in Osaka shipped a box called the TB-303 Bass Line. It was aimed at guitarists who rehearsed alone and needed a bass player. It was not a very good bass player. It couldn't hold a tuning, it couldn't be programmed from the stage, and its filter made everything it played sound like a small waterfowl being strangled. Roland sold ten thousand of them over eighteen months, discontinued the product, and moved on.

Six years later, in Chicago, three teenagers — DJ Pierre, Spanky, and Herb J — bought a used 303 for fifty dollars from a music store, couldn't figure out how to program it "correctly," accidentally left the resonance high and the cutoff moving, and recorded the sixteen-minute jam that became *Acid Tracks* under the name Phuture. DJ Pierre has said in interviews, repeatedly, that they didn't think of it as a bass line at all. They thought of it as a new sound. They couldn't have named the genre that sound would found, because the genre did not exist yet.

Acid house is what happens when a discarded tool is picked up the wrong way by people who are not in a position to be told off for holding it wrong. That is the whole story. Every chapter that follows is a footnote to it.

## The argument of this book

Most writing about the 303 focuses on one of three things: the circuit, the history, or the subculture. The circuit lives in synthesizer textbooks and circuit-bender forums. The history lives in oral histories from Chicago, Detroit, London, and Berlin. The subculture — rave, acieed, Second Summer of Love, chemical generation — lives in sociology papers and Mixmag retrospectives.

This book is about the fourth thing: how the instrument becomes a musical idea. How the specific decisions you make on the 303's controls — *more resonance or less? accent this step or not? slide into the root or approach it by rest?* — become a melodic and rhythmic vocabulary. Why some of those decisions sound like *Acid Tracks* and some sound like *Voodoo Ray* and some sound like the sort of demo you never finish.

We will spend a lot of time on the knobs. Not because the 303 is complicated — it isn't — but because the 303 is *constrained*. There are only a few controls, they interact, and the space of musical results is therefore unusually shallow in the trivial directions and unusually deep in the important ones. To get good at acid is to get good at a very small number of moves. This book is about those moves.

## What you'll be able to do by the end

Ambitious list, but real:

- **Hear** an acid record and identify, by ear, roughly what the cutoff is doing over the course of a 32-bar section, whether resonance is being adjusted live, where the accents fall, and whether slides are part of the pattern's structure or its ornamentation.
- **Place** an acid record within its subgenre — Chicago acid, Detroit acid, acid techno, electro acid, new-beat — and explain what structural features do the placing.
- **Build** a 303 pattern from a blank state without resorting to the randomizer, using the conventions of the genre rather than random exploration.
- **Evolve** that pattern across a five-minute track using only the tools a real producer would have had: filter moves, accent edits, pattern swaps, FX sends. No DAW tricks. No post-production.
- **Speak** the working vocabulary of the scene — portamento, ghost note, p-lock, dub-send, risers — without feeling like you're faking it.

You will also, if you read the later chapters carefully, be able to finish a track. That is a more ambitious goal than it sounds. Acid tends to reward the first hour of work and punish the next ten. Most acid demos are good. Most acid records are mediocre. The difference is arrangement, and arrangement is learnable; it just isn't glamorous. We will learn it.

## What acid is not

A few disclaimers before we start, because acid attracts mythology and mythology attracts wrong teaching.

**Acid is not a filter sweep.** A slow cutoff ramp on a 303 pattern is the *clichéd* gesture of acid, but it's not what makes acid *acid*. Acid is the relationship between the resonance, the envelope, the accent circuit, and the slide — all of which produce sound even when the cutoff is nailed in place. If you turn off everything except the cutoff, you have a filter demo, not an acid record.

**Acid is not a genre defined by the instrument.** There is plenty of 303 in records that are not acid (early Detroit techno, lots of big beat, Prodigy's entire catalog). There are plenty of acid records that don't use a 303 at all (anything by Hardfloor after 1998, most of the modern acid-techno catalog, a lot of Plastikman). Acid is a *sound-world* and a *compositional approach* that the 303 happened to precipitate. We will talk about how to reproduce the sound-world on acidflow, but we will not pretend that owning acidflow makes your music acid any more than owning a Stratocaster makes your music surf rock.

**Acid is not a joke.** There is a tendency, especially online, to treat acid as a formalist stunt — three notes and a filter sweep for nine minutes. If you listen to the records that way, they sound dumb. If you listen to the records the way they were meant to be listened to — at four in the morning, on a rig bigger than your apartment, inside a crowd that has been dancing for three hours — the three notes become a mantra, the filter sweep becomes a breath, and the nine minutes become a pilgrimage. This book assumes you want to make music that rewards that kind of listening, not music that rewards a critical essay.

## How to read this book

Thirty-nine chapters plus some reference material is a lot. You do not have to read it all in order. A few paths through:

- **Total beginner.** Read Part I end-to-end. Skip Part II. Jump to Chapter 15 (the sequencer grid), then Chapters 22–25 (drums). Come back to Part II one chapter at a time as you run into things you don't understand. Then Part VII if you want to make actual tracks.

- **Synth-literate, new to acid.** Skim Chapter 5 (synthesis). Read Chapter 6 (what makes the 303 the 303) carefully — it explains the non-obvious stuff. Read Chapter 12 (accent) twice. Read Part VII end-to-end.

- **Acid nerd, new to acidflow.** Part II, Chapters 7–8 to get oriented. Chapter 19 (probability/ratchet), Chapter 20 (p-locks), Chapter 33 (randomizer) — these are things the original 303 didn't have and that acidflow does. The rest you already know; read it for pleasure or for corrections.

- **I just want to make a track tonight.** Chapter 37. Come back for the theory later.
