# 39 · The 16 built-in presets annotated

acidflow ships with 16 presets — pre-programmed patterns + knob settings that recreate or evoke canonical acid tracks. This chapter walks through each one, explaining the pattern, the knob settings, and what you can learn from it.

These are plausible reconstructions. The original sequencer data for most of these tracks isn't publicly documented; the presets come from community consensus (Attack Magazine tutorials, KVR forums, Robin Whittle's circuit analysis) and aim to evoke the feel when paired with the right knobs.

Loading a preset overwrites the current pattern. To load: scroll through presets in the Transport section, or use mouse scroll in the preset browser area.

## Preset 1: Acid Tracks

**Inspired by**: Phuture, *Acid Tracks* (1987).

**What the pattern is**: Pedal point on C (MIDI 48). Most steps play C. Two steps (specifically, step 3 and step 11 in most reconstructions) jump up an octave to C5. Those octave-up notes are accented and slide into the following note.

**Knob recipe**: CUTOFF mid-low (start around 25%), RES around 70%, ENVMOD around 55%, DECAY around 45%. These will be adjusted over time.

**The trick**: the song's entire identity is the *cutoff sweep*. Slowly raise CUTOFF over many bars. The pattern stays the same; the tonal character evolves.

**What to learn from it**: that a minimal pattern can support a 12-minute track through nothing but filter motion. You don't need complex melodies.

**Try**: load the preset, play for a full minute without touching anything, then slowly sweep CUTOFF from 25% to 80% over another minute.

## Preset 2: Higher State

**Inspired by**: Josh Wink, *Higher State of Consciousness* (1995).

**What the pattern is**: Simple pedal/melodic hybrid. Root notes with occasional color tones. Deliberately simple — the track's interest is in the arrangement and filter motion, not in the pattern itself.

**Knob recipe**: Similar to Acid Tracks but with DECAY a bit longer. Open CUTOFF slowly across 32+ bars.

**The trick**: patience. Wink's track develops over minutes, not seconds. You have to resist the urge to tweak quickly.

**What to learn**: slow filter motion over long timeframes is expressive.

## Preset 3: Acperience

**Inspired by**: Hardfloor, *Acperience 1* (1992).

**What the pattern is**: A-minor key. Heavy accents on downbeats. Octave-plus slides back to root — the signature "scream then fall" Hardfloor move. Specifically: an accented C6 or D6 slides down to A3 (root). The slide covers a large interval and drops the listener back to the root.

**Knob recipe**: High RES (80%+), relatively low CUTOFF, short DECAY, ENVMOD high. This produces the self-oscillating scream on accented notes.

**The trick**: the scream comes from pushing the filter into self-oscillation on accent. The large slide interval makes the pattern feel dramatic.

**What to learn**: accents + slides + self-oscillation = Hardfloor.

**Try**: load it, play, raise RES further to 95% and listen to the scream get more pronounced.

## Preset 4: No Way Back

**Inspired by**: Adonis, *No Way Back* (1986).

**What the pattern is**: A call-and-response in C. Step 1-4 = C pedal. Step 5-8 = a brief melodic phrase (C to Eb, or C to F, or similar). Then back to C pedal. Step 13-16 = another variation.

**Knob recipe**: Classic settings. CUTOFF moderate, RES medium-high, ENVMOD medium.

**The trick**: this is proto-melodic acid. The line has a SHAPE beyond "pedal on root" — there's an actual musical phrase.

**What to learn**: you can have a recognizable riff even in acid. The pattern doesn't have to be pedal-only.

## Preset 5: Voodoo Ray

**Inspired by**: A Guy Called Gerald, *Voodoo Ray* (1988).

**What the pattern is**: A minor pentatonic melody. Notes like A, C, D, E, G in varying order. Slides between several notes give the "sung" character.

**Knob recipe**: *Low* RES (below 50%) — this is crucial. Open CUTOFF. Short-to-medium DECAY.

**The trick**: RES under 50% means no screaming. The 303 sounds more like a melodic bass than a filter toy. Gerald's choice to dial down resonance is what makes this track "melodic" vs "acid."

**What to learn**: acid doesn't have to scream. A musical, vocal 303 is possible and beautiful.

## Preset 6: Energy Flash

**Inspired by**: Joey Beltram, *Energy Flash* (1990).

**What the pattern is**: Every step plays E (MIDI 40 or E1 / E2 depending on octave). 16 hits per bar, all the same pitch. Accents on downbeats (1, 5, 9, 13).

**Knob recipe**: Heavy DRIVE. Moderate RES. CUTOFF starts low, opens progressively.

**The trick**: this is a drone, not a melody. The "musical" content is entirely in the filter motion and the accent pattern. Beltram discovered that you don't need melody to have acid — you need texture.

**What to learn**: rhythm + texture + filter = a valid track shape. Melody optional.

## Preset 7: Plastikman

**Inspired by**: Richie Hawtin's Plastikman alias.

**What the pattern is**: Sparse. Maybe 5-7 hits per bar. Long decays so each note rings. Pitches range but usually in a minor pentatonic.

**Knob recipe**: Long DECAY. High RES. Variable CUTOFF — key is slow motion. ACCENT moderate. DRIVE low.

**The trick**: the space between notes is as important as the notes themselves. Long decays mean each note bleeds into the next.

**What to learn**: minimalism as composition. Less is more.

## Preset 8: Hoover Dub

**Inspired by**: Generic dub-techno hoover bass.

**What the pattern is**: Extremely sparse. Maybe 3-4 hits per bar, all on the root or octave. Long decays.

**Knob recipe**: Long DECAY (maxed). Low CUTOFF. Moderate RES. BPM slow (could be 60-80 BPM for half-time feel).

**The trick**: it's more a drone than a melody. The 303 is a sustained low tone with minor variations.

**What to learn**: the 303 can be used as ambient/dub material, not just dance.

## Preset 9: Squelch (Showcase)

**Inspired by**: not a specific track — a demonstration of heavy resonance.

**What the pattern is**: Single-octave jumps stress the accent envelope stack. Specifically: consecutive accented notes build up the accent capacitor, producing rising squelch.

**Knob recipe**: Heavy RES. Heavy ACCENT. Short DECAY. Every other step accented.

**The trick**: accent stacking. Accent capacitor doesn't fully discharge between notes. Consecutive accents layer.

**What to learn**: the accent envelope is cumulative. Use it.

## Preset 10: Detroit

**Inspired by**: Underground Resistance / Detroit techno.

**What the pattern is**: Rolling A-minor bassline. Even, 16th-note feel. Every step plays. Mostly root and octave.

**Knob recipe**: Open CUTOFF. Moderate RES. Driving feel.

**The trick**: relentless. Every step plays. Like Energy Flash but with more pitch variation.

**What to learn**: Detroit acid-techno is about forward motion, not stops.

## Preset 11: Dub 303

**Inspired by**: Dub techno bass lines.

**What the pattern is**: Half-time feel. Very sparse. Long echoes from FX.

**Knob recipe**: BPM slow. DECAY long. FX delay and reverb heavy.

**The trick**: let the FX carry the track. The 303 pattern is almost nothing; the delay/reverb do the work.

**What to learn**: FX can be the primary compositional element, not just decoration.

## Preset 12: Liquid (Showcase)

**Inspired by**: demonstration of slide behavior.

**What the pattern is**: Every step has a slide. The entire pattern is one continuous glide from note to note. The envelope never retriggers (slides suppress retrigger).

**Knob recipe**: Moderate CUTOFF, moderate RES, long DECAY (to match the continuous feel).

**The trick**: the envelope starts high (from the first non-slide note) and slowly decays over many steps because nothing retriggers.

**What to learn**: slides suppress envelope retriggering. Chains of slides create sustained filter motion.

## Preset 13: Humanoid

**Inspired by**: Stakker / Humanoid, *Stakker Humanoid* (1988).

**What the pattern is**: FSOL-era busy pattern. Octave hops on every step. Melodic and dense.

**Knob recipe**: Moderate settings. Moderate RES. Balanced.

**The trick**: alternating octaves. Step 1 root, step 2 root+12, step 3 root, step 4 root+12. Creates a "zipper" feel.

**What to learn**: regular octave alternation is its own pattern type.

## Preset 14: Melodic (Showcase)

**Inspired by**: demonstration of a full A-minor melody.

**What the pattern is**: A longer phrased line, pseudo-arpeggio through A minor. 8-bar phrasing compressed into one 16-step pattern.

**Knob recipe**: Open CUTOFF. Modest RES (to hear the melody, not the scream). Long DECAY.

**The trick**: the pattern works as a melody even without the filter character. You could sing it.

**What to learn**: 303 patterns can be proper melodies, not just pedal points.

## Preset 15: Bassline

**Inspired by**: UK bassline genre.

**What the pattern is**: Syncopated, swung. Notes on unexpected beats.

**Knob recipe**: Moderate. SWING at 60%+. BPM around 140.

**The trick**: the swing is what makes it feel UK bassline. Without swing, it's just syncopation.

**What to learn**: swing transforms any pattern. Apply with care.

## Preset 16: Empty

**Inspired by**: a blank slate.

**What the pattern is**: All rests. Nothing plays.

**Knob recipe**: Default knob positions.

**The trick**: no trick. Start from scratch.

**What to learn**: sometimes the best preset is the one you program yourself.

## How to use the presets

Presets are not finished tracks — they're starting points. Load one, play it, get a feel, then:

1. **Randomize from the preset**. Press `r`. You're near the preset's character but with variation.
2. **Mutate the preset**. Press `M` a few times. Small changes from the preset base.
3. **Re-program over the preset**. Keep the knob settings, rewrite the pattern. The pattern is the fastest thing to change; keeping the knobs lets you keep the "sound."

Or just use the preset as-is for learning:

1. Load preset.
2. Study the pattern. Identify accents, slides, rests.
3. Turn knobs and listen to how each affects the preset.
4. Export and compare to the reference recording.

## Creating your own presets

There's no built-in "save preset" feature. But you can save to slots (`Shift+1` through `Shift+9`). Slots store the pattern (including drums) but NOT the knob positions.

To "save a preset" informally:

1. Set up knobs.
2. Write the pattern.
3. Save pattern to a slot (`Shift+N`).
4. Write down your knob settings (on paper, or in a text file).
5. To recall: load the slot, then manually set the knobs to your written-down values.

It's not elegant. Maybe a future version of acidflow will save knobs in slots.

## Sharing patterns

Use the text export (`p` key). Writes the pattern to `~/.config/acidflow/pattern.txt`. Share the file — the recipient can load it with `Shift+P`.

This is how you pass a pattern to someone else. They still need to set their own knobs.

1. Load each preset in order (1 through 15).
2. For each: play for 30 seconds.
3. Note: what's the 303 doing? What's the pattern's character? What era does it evoke?
4. Pick your favorite. Study its pattern. Try to reverse-engineer what the producer did.
