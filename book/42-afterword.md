# 42 · Afterword and further listening

You've reached the end. 42 chapters of acid theory, 303 design quirks, sequencer programming, drum conventions, FX routing, arrangement structures, subgenre signatures, and mixing advice. If you read them all and did the "Try this" exercises, you now know more about acid music than most working producers.

That's not a boast. Most working producers learn by osmosis — they buy gear, copy records, iterate. This book tried to make the knowledge explicit. Having it explicit means you can:

- Make faster decisions (you know *why* to reach for a particular knob).
- Produce in unfamiliar subgenres (you have the vocabulary for Hardfloor, Plastikman, UK acid, Chicago, etc.).
- Teach someone else (you can articulate what you're doing).

Now what?

## Now you make records

The book is finite. Your practice is ongoing. Everything in this book is a tool; tools matter only as much as you use them.

Target: make 100 acid tracks. Not polished, published records — 100 finished, exported WAV files. Each one teaches you something. By track 30, you'll have stylistic preferences. By track 60, you'll have technical fluency. By track 100, you'll be genuinely good.

Don't try to shortcut this. There's no book that replaces 100 hours of hands-on practice. The book reduces wasted hours — it won't eliminate them.

## Reading list

Books specifically on acid:

- *Energy Flash* by Simon Reynolds (2013). History of rave, covering acid in context.
- *Last Night a DJ Saved My Life* by Bill Brewster and Frank Broughton. Broader DJ history with acid chapters.
- Robin Whittle's online *firstpr.com.au/rwi/dfish/303* — detailed technical analysis of the TB-303 circuit.

Books on synthesis generally:

- *Welsh's Synthesizer Cookbook* by Fred Welsh. Classic patch recipes.
- *Computer Music Tutorial* by Curtis Roads. Deep DSP.
- *Designing Sound* by Andy Farnell. Synthesis theory and practice.

Articles:

- Attack Magazine's 303 tutorials (multiple).
- Sound on Sound archives on the TB-303.
- DSP PDFs by Pirkle, Zavalishin, Stinchcombe.

## On acidflow itself

acidflow is a personal project. It will evolve. Features I'm considering for future versions:

- **Per-element FX sends** (so drums and 303 can have different delay/reverb amounts).
- **More drum voices** (toms, cymbals, percussion).
- **LFO modulation** of knobs.
- **Sidechain compression**.
- **Cross-platform MIDI** (macOS, Windows).
- **Multiple 303 voices** (or polyphonic 303).
- **Per-slot knob saves**.
- **Undo / history**.

Some of these may never arrive. Some may. acidflow is shaped by use. If you have requests or suggestions, the repo welcomes them.

Especially welcome:

## The last word

Acid is a strange genre. It was invented accidentally. Its signature instrument was a commercial failure. Its original producers didn't fully understand what they were doing. Its first records were considered novelties.

Forty years later, acid is a living genre. New acid tracks come out every week. New subgenres emerge. The TB-303 has been cloned dozens of times, including in software, including in acidflow.

This is because acid contains something genuinely weird. The filter + accent + slide combination produces sounds that don't exist in other synths and don't sound like other instruments. Producers keep rediscovering that weirdness.

You're now part of that tradition. Take the knowledge in this book and make something no one else has made. Or make something that sounds like Phuture but is yours. Or abandon all the conventions and just have fun.

Whatever you do: make records.

Make them often.

Make them badly at first.

Make them better over time.

Release what you make.

The world has too few acid producers. The community needs yours.

Thanks for reading.

— The author
